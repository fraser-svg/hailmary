## Dean & Wiseman Memo Doctrine

**Panel-Reviewed Condensed Guidance**

This is the **operating doctrine** for every Dean & Wiseman memo.
If a memo does not follow these principles, it should not be sent.

---

# 1. The Objective

The memo is not outreach.

It is **strategic intelligence delivered to a founder**.

The founder’s reaction must be:

> “How do these people understand our company this well?”

If it reads like marketing, consulting, or advice — it has failed.

---

# 2. The Three Effects (Non-Negotiable)

Every memo must create three reactions in the founder:

### Recognition

The founder instantly recognises the pattern.

> “That is exactly what’s happening.”

### Surprise

The memo frames the issue in a way they have not articulated.

> “I’ve never seen it described like that.”

### Curiosity

The memo implies a solution without fully explaining it.

> “What do they mean by that intervention?”

If all three occur, the meeting happens naturally.

---

# 3. The Core Insight

Every memo revolves around a **Narrative Gap**.

A narrative gap is the difference between:

```
How the company positions the product
vs
How the market actually experiences it
```

The pattern usually looks like:

```
Product experienced as X
Product positioned as Y
```

That mismatch alters:

* who evaluates the product
* how deals move
* where adoption stalls

The memo exposes this pattern.

---

# 4. Evidence Standard

Authority comes from **signal density**.

Every memo must contain **3–5 concrete signals**, preferably verbatim fragments:

* customer review excerpts
* developer discussions
* community threads
* documentation signals
* pricing structure
* competitor positioning

Example:

> A G2 reviewer describes the product as “the fastest way to automate vendor onboarding.”
> Yet the homepage positions the product as procurement infrastructure.

Fragments create the sense that the company has been **studied carefully**.

---

# 5. Diagnosis Discipline

Narrative gaps typically fall into a small set of structural patterns:

**Positioning gap**
Product described differently by customers than by the company.

**ICP mismatch**
Product wins with a different buyer than the stated ICP.

**Proof gap**
Buyers lack the evidence required to justify purchase.

**Category error**
Product framed in the wrong market category.

**Distribution misalignment**
Product discovered in a channel different from where deals close.

Correct diagnosis determines credibility.

---

# 6. The Memo Structure

Target length: **650–850 words**

### Observation

Concrete external signals describing how the product is perceived.

### The Pattern

Explicitly name the narrative gap.

Example:

> The product is experienced as a speed tool.
> It is positioned as procurement infrastructure.

### What This Means

Explain the commercial consequence.

Example:

> Infrastructure products are evaluated by procurement.
> Speed tools are adopted by operators.
> The current positioning shifts the buying process into a slower path.

### Why This Happens

Explain the two structural forces behind the gap.

Usually:

* founder’s original framing
* market’s actual use case

### What This Changes

Describe the **strategic lever implied by the evidence**.

Do not fully reveal the solution.

Example:

> Companies that resolve this pattern typically change the narrative anchor — leading with the outcome customers already describe.

### Next Step

A diagnostic CTA.

Example:

> If the diagnosis is wrong, it would be useful to know.
> If it’s right, there is a specific way companies resolve it.
> Twenty minutes is enough to test which it is.

---

# 7. Writing Principles

Tone: **internal strategy document**

Rules:

* restrained
* precise
* analytical
* no hype
* no flattery
* no urgency
* no marketing language

Every sentence must contain **company-specific information**.

If a sentence could appear in a memo about another company, remove it.

---

# 8. Evidence over Opinion

The memo must feel like **research**, not interpretation.

Bad:

> Customers seem to value speed.

Good:

> Multiple reviews emphasise onboarding speed as the primary benefit.

Specific signals build authority.

---

# 9. The Pattern Reveal

The memo succeeds or fails on the **Pattern section**.

It must be simple and unmistakable:

```
Customers describe the product as X.
The company presents it as Y.
```

Once the founder sees this, the rest of the memo becomes obvious.

---

# 10. The Intervention Principle

Never fully explain the solution.

The memo should reveal **the lever**, not the implementation.

This preserves curiosity and creates the reason to speak.

---

# 11. The CTA Principle

The CTA should not feel like a meeting request.

It should feel like **continuing the analysis**.

Best format:

> If the diagnosis is wrong, it would be useful to know.
> If it is right, there is a specific intervention that resolves it.
> Twenty minutes is enough to test which it is.

---

# 12. The Strategic Advantage

Dean & Wiseman’s advantage is not writing.

It is **systematic narrative gap detection**.

The memo is simply the **delivery mechanism**.

Most outreach tries to persuade.

Dean & Wiseman reveals **structural truth about the company’s growth**.

That difference is what causes founders to respond.
