# Expected Patterns — Fixture 001: AI Services

## Must Detect

### 1. Service-assisted delivery beneath automation narrative

Multiple tensions converge on the same structural form: the company markets an autonomous AI product, but its actual delivery mechanism depends on human services at every observable touchpoint — onboarding, implementation, configuration, and ongoing support.

This is not a single tension. It is a recurring structural pattern across:

- marketing vs customer experience (automation_vs_service tension)
- pricing vs product positioning (claim_vs_reality tension)
- hiring priorities vs stated investment narrative (credibility_vs_claim tension)

The pattern suggests the automation narrative is a positioning layer over a fundamentally service-dependent operating model.

Constituent tensions: automation_vs_service, claim_vs_reality, positioning_vs_delivery

### 2. Narrative-operating model mismatch

The company's external narrative (AI-first, technology-led, scalable) is structurally misaligned with its operating model (services-heavy, consultant-dependent, linear scaling).

This pattern is visible across:

- external press framing vs internal employee perception
- CEO's stated priorities vs actual resource allocation
- product marketing vs customer-described experience

The mismatch is not incidental — it appears systematic, touching marketing, pricing, hiring, and leadership communication.

Constituent tensions: positioning_vs_delivery, vision_vs_execution, credibility_vs_claim

## Nice to Detect

### 3. Customer value attribution pattern

Across multiple independent customer sources (G2, Capterra, case study), customers consistently attribute value to human support rather than AI capability. This is not one review — it is a pattern across source types.

This pattern strengthens the service-dependency hypothesis by showing it from the customer's perspective rather than just from operational signals.

### 4. Hiring-as-strategy-reveal pattern

The company's hiring activity reveals strategic priorities more accurately than its public messaging. Services roles outnumber engineering hires, professional services is being formalized as a department, and solutions engineering is being scaled — all signals of a service-led business.

## Must Avoid

### Single-tension restatement

A pattern is not a restatement of one tension. If the system produces "there is a tension between automation claims and service delivery" as a pattern, it has failed to synthesize across multiple tensions.

### Industry-level patterns

"AI companies often overpromise automation" is an industry observation, not a company-specific pattern derived from this dossier's signals and tensions.
