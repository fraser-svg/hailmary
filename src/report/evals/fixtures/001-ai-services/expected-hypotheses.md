# Expected Hypotheses — Fixture 001: AI Services

## Must Detect

### 1. Automation narrative compensates for immature product automation

The company's strong automation messaging may exist because the underlying product cannot yet deliver autonomous operation. The narrative fills the gap between where the product is and where the company wants the market to believe it is.

Supporting pattern: service-assisted delivery beneath automation narrative

This hypothesis explains why:
- customers describe manual onboarding despite automation claims
- implementation specialists are the fastest-growing role
- pricing includes mandatory service fees for what is marketed as self-serve

### 2. Human onboarding is required because product automation is incomplete

The product may be unable to deliver value without significant human configuration and training. The onboarding program is not a temporary growth-stage artifact — it is a structural requirement of the current product's capability level.

Supporting pattern: narrative-operating model mismatch

This hypothesis explains why:
- case studies describe 12-week consulting engagements
- customers credit human consultants for outcomes
- the company is formalizing professional services as a department rather than sunsetting it

## Acceptable Alternatives

The reasoning engine may generate alternative or complementary hypotheses. These are acceptable if well-supported.

### A. Services-led GTM is intentional, not compensatory

The company may deliberately use high-touch onboarding as a competitive moat and land-and-expand strategy, while maintaining the AI narrative for market positioning purposes. The gap between narrative and delivery could be strategic rather than a product maturity issue.

### B. Automation narrative is aspirational and forward-looking

The company may be describing its product roadmap as its current capability — common in early-stage VC-backed companies. The services dependency may be a known temporary state with a credible automation roadmap.

### C. Two-speed delivery model

The product may genuinely automate some functions (e.g., send-time optimization, basic sequencing) while requiring human expertise for higher-order configuration (e.g., strategy design, integration architecture). The automation claims may be partially true but overstated in scope.

## Must Avoid

### Deterministic conclusions

- "AutoFlow is a services company disguised as a SaaS company."
- Hypotheses must remain explanatory possibilities, not verdicts.

### Unsupported speculation

- "The CEO is deliberately misleading investors about AI capabilities."
- This requires intent evidence not present in the dossier. The hypothesis stage must not infer motive without evidence.

### Industry platitudes

- "Many AI startups struggle with the gap between promise and delivery."
- Hypotheses must be specific to this company's evidence, not generic industry commentary.

### Monocausal explanations

- Hypotheses should acknowledge that multiple explanations may be partially true. The system should avoid presenting a single hypothesis as definitively correct.
