# Expected Patterns — Fixture 002: Enterprise Proof Gap

## Must Detect

### 1. Enterprise aspiration exceeding observable enterprise adoption

Multiple tensions converge on the same structural form: the company projects enterprise identity across marketing, product features, and press, but no dimension of the observable evidence — customers, pricing, hiring, case studies — demonstrates enterprise-scale adoption.

This is not a single mismatch. It is a recurring pattern across:

- positioning vs customer base (enterprise_positioning_vs_customer_reality tension)
- ambition vs proof (ambition_vs_proof tension)
- narrative scale vs operational structure (narrative_scale_vs_operational_evidence tension)

The pattern suggests the enterprise narrative is aspirational rather than descriptive of current reality.

Constituent tensions: enterprise_positioning_vs_customer_reality, ambition_vs_proof

### 2. Narrative-operating model mismatch around customer scale

The company's external narrative targets enterprise, but its operating model — pricing, sales motion, customer success structure — is built for SMB. This is visible across:

- pricing tiers designed for 5-50 user teams
- sales roles targeting $5K-$25K deals with 2-4 week cycles
- CS portfolios of 80-120 accounts (SMB model, not enterprise)
- case studies featuring 8-12 person companies

The mismatch is systematic: marketing, press, and product positioning all say "enterprise," while operational structure, hiring, pricing, and customer evidence all say "SMB."

Constituent tensions: enterprise_positioning_vs_customer_reality, positioning_vs_market_fit

## Nice to Detect

### 3. Positioning-led growth strategy

The company may be using enterprise positioning as a top-of-funnel credibility signal while serving SMB customers. This pattern — leading with enterprise narrative to attract interest, then converting SMB buyers — is a recognizable growth strategy that the evidence supports but does not confirm.

## Must Avoid

### Single-tension restatement

A pattern is not a restatement of one tension. If the system produces "enterprise positioning does not match customer reality" as a pattern, it has failed to synthesize across multiple tensions and domains.

### Industry-level patterns

"Many startups position as enterprise before achieving enterprise adoption" is an industry observation, not a company-specific pattern derived from this dossier's signals and tensions.

### Causal explanations

"The company cannot serve enterprise because its product lacks key capabilities." Patterns observe structural form — they do not explain causation or attribute blame.
