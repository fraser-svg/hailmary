# Expected Hypotheses — Fixture 002: Enterprise Proof Gap

## Must Detect

### 1. Enterprise positioning may be aspirational rather than reflective of the current customer base

The company's consistent use of "enterprise" across marketing, product, and press may represent where the company wants to be, not where it currently is. The absence of enterprise customer evidence — combined with SMB-oriented pricing, hiring, and case studies — suggests the enterprise narrative is forward-looking positioning, not a description of current reality.

Supporting pattern: enterprise aspiration exceeding observable enterprise adoption

This hypothesis explains why:
- all published case studies feature small companies (8-12 people)
- pricing is structured for SMB/mid-market adoption
- customer reviews frame the product in SMB competitive terms
- no enterprise logos or references are observable

### 2. The company may be targeting enterprise credibility while building traction in smaller organizations

The enterprise positioning may be a deliberate signal to investors, press, and the market to establish credibility, while the actual customer acquisition focuses on SMB organizations. The product may genuinely serve small teams well, with the enterprise narrative serving a branding function rather than a customer acquisition function.

Supporting pattern: narrative-operating model mismatch around customer scale

This hypothesis explains why:
- press coverage uses enterprise framing (reinforces brand credibility)
- hiring targets SMB deal sizes and CS portfolios (actual customer motion)
- customers describe SMB-appropriate value (quick setup, simplicity)
- CEO positions against legacy enterprise tools (aspirational competitor set)

## Acceptable Alternatives

The reasoning engine may generate alternative or complementary hypotheses. These are acceptable if well-supported.

### A. The enterprise segment may be early in adoption

The company may have genuine enterprise customers or pipeline that are not yet visible in public evidence. Enterprise sales cycles are long, and the absence of enterprise proof may reflect timing rather than capability. The SMB traction may be the visible portion of a broader adoption curve.

### B. SMB adoption may be a deliberate beachhead strategy

The company may be executing a bottom-up strategy: build adoption in small teams, demonstrate value, and expand upmarket. The enterprise positioning may be intentional priming for a future upmarket motion rather than a misrepresentation of the current state.

### C. Enterprise features exist to close SMB deals at higher price points

The SOC 2, SSO, and RBAC features may serve a different purpose than true enterprise readiness. They may function as checkbox items that help close mid-market deals where procurement requires security documentation, even if the actual deployments are small.

## Must Avoid

### Accusations of deception

- "StratusFlow is deliberately misleading the market about its enterprise capabilities."
- Hypotheses must not infer intent to deceive. Enterprise aspirational positioning is common and may be strategic.

### Deterministic conclusions about company failure

- "StratusFlow will never succeed in enterprise."
- Hypotheses must remain explanatory possibilities, not predictions or verdicts.

### Recommendations

- "The company should stop calling itself enterprise and rebrand as an SMB tool."
- Hypotheses explain observed patterns. They do not prescribe strategy.

### Industry platitudes

- "Many startups claim to be enterprise before they are ready."
- Hypotheses must be specific to this company's evidence, not generic commentary.

### Monocausal explanations

- Hypotheses should acknowledge that multiple explanations may be partially true. Enterprise positioning could be simultaneously aspirational, strategic, and premature.
